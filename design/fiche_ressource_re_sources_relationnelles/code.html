Navigating Workplace Burnout: A Practical Guide to Recovery

Dr. Sarah Jenkins
Clinical Psychologist

Burnout is more than just feeling tired after a long week. It's a state of emotional, physical, and mental exhaustion caused by excessive and prolonged stress. It occurs when you feel overwhelmed, emotionally drained, and unable to meet constant demands.

Recognizing the Early Signs

Often, we ignore the subtle signals our bodies and minds send us. The journey to recovery begins with acknowledgment. If you find yourself increasingly cynical about your job, lacking the energy to be consistently productive, or feeling a sense of dread when waking up, these are not just "bad days"—they are indicators of a systemic issue.

"Recovery is not about bouncing back to the way things were. It's about building a new, more sustainable way of living and working."

Actionable Steps for Today

While deep recovery takes time, there are immediate interventions that can stabilize your daily experience. Creating strict boundaries between "work time" and "personal time" is paramount. This means disabling notifications, physically closing your workspace if possible, and engaging in transition rituals.

  • done Implement a Hard Stop: Choose a specific time to end your workday and stick to it relentlessly.
  • done Micro-Restorations: Take 5-minute sensory breaks away from screens every 90 minutes.
  • done Delegate or Drop: Audit your current tasks and identify at least one that can be delayed or delegated.
